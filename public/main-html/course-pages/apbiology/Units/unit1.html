Structure of Water and Hydrogen Bonding

Water is a polar molecule composed of two hydrogen atoms bonded to one oxygen atom, forming a bent shape. This polarity enables hydrogen bonding between water molecules, where the slightly positive hydrogen of one molecule attracts the slightly negative oxygen of another. These hydrogen bonds give water unique properties like high surface tension, cohesion, adhesion, and the ability to moderate temperature - all essential for supporting life.

Elements of Life

The essential elements of life—carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and sulfur (S)—form the building blocks of all biological molecules. Carbon's unique bonding ability creates complex structures like proteins and DNA, while hydrogen and oxygen compose water. Nitrogen and phosphorus are vital for genetic material and energy transfer (ATP), and sulfur stabilizes proteins. Together, these elements enable life's chemical reactions, structure, and function.

Introduction to Biological Macromolecules (BM)

Biological macromolecules—carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids—are large molecules essential for life. Made of smaller repeating units, they perform critical functions: carbs provide energy, lipids store energy and form membranes, proteins enable reactions and build structures, and nucleic acids store genetic information. Together, they form the chemical foundation of all living systems.

Properties of BM

Biological macromolecules (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, nucleic acids) exhibit unique properties that enable life. Carbohydrates provide quick energy and structural support through glycosidic bonds. Lipids store long-term energy (fats) and form hydrophobic barriers (phospholipids). Proteins fold into specific 3D shapes for catalytic, structural, and signaling functions via peptide bonds. Nucleic acids store and transmit genetic information through complementary base pairing. Their structure-function relationships drive all cellular processes.

Structure and Function of BM

Biological macromolecules' structures directly determine their functions: carbohydrates (sugar chains) provide energy and structure; lipids (hydrophobic molecules) store energy and form membranes; proteins (folded amino acid chains) enable reactions and build tissues; nucleic acids (nucleotide chains) store and transmit genetic information. Each molecule's unique architecture drives its specific role in sustaining life.

Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) are macromolecules composed of nucleotide monomers that store and transmit genetic information. DNA's double-stranded helical structure provides stable genetic storage, while RNA's single-stranded form enables protein synthesis and gene regulation. Their sequence of bases (A,T/U,C,G) encodes the instructions for all life processes.
